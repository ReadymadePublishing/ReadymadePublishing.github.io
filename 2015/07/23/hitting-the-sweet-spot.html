Editorial

I'm on holiday this week, so haven't been able to trawl through the week's Subbuteo news. Don't worry - there'll be a full update next week - but in the meantime, here's an exclusive interview with incoming ASA president Chris Sweetland.

Hitting the Sweet spot

Part of a delegation of 8 players from the USA going to Italy for the World Cup this September represents the culmination of a Subbuteo career lasting for 42 years - with a large break in the middle - for incoming ASA president Chris Sweetland.

A childhood friend who had returned to the Chicago from London brought the game back with him. The pair played for hours every day, playing by an improvised set of rules, isolated from the rest of the community. “We were the only two players we were aware of in the area.”, says Chris, “We were really not too aware of what was going on with the game in the United States or internationally, and we really did not care.” The pair maintained detailed statistics on every figure in on their team, publishing a sports paper with colourful match reports.

Several years passed, by which time they had subscribed to a Subbuteo newsletter, becoming aware of the popularity of the game overseas, although they were unaware of anyone else in the area that played. Then, after graduating from high school, Chris left for college, with the intention of finishing his involvement with the game. “We would get together occasionally and I would play with my two brothers but I put the game away for what I thought was forever when I graduated high school”.

Then, two years ago, Chris returned. “I knocked around for about 2 months before deciding to search the game on-line to find out what had become of it”, he says. “I was almost sure, at the time, that the game had died a slow death.”. Instead he found that not only was the same still going, but there was some relatively local action too, with a group of players a three hour drive away, in Michigan. There were also small groups throughout the country, each with similar stories to his their own.

In a typically upbeat statement, Chris describes how the Chicago club that he’d founded has blossomed: “Our club decided to host the 2015 ASA National Tournament in August as a way to gain national visibility as a club that wants to see the sport grow”. Within months he had become an active organiser in the Midwest, becoming ASA Vice President for the region. He is due to become ASA President in September this year.

He’s very positive, too, about the benefits that Twitter, Facebook, and internet forums can bring: “Social media has been really good for the game. It allows us to see what is happening around the world in real-time. It really helped promote and keep the game vibrant in the United States as well since there is such a large geographic divide between the active pockets of Subbuteo players”. He also sees this as the future of the American game, providing a way to bring younger players in to an association with an ageing membership.

He’s no less enthusiastic about the role that FISTF, WASPA and the American Subbuteo Association have played, continuing “I liked the fact that a broadly accepted set of rule changes came out of this effort [by FISTF] recently many of which I think add to the enjoyment and playability of the game.”

Whilst Chris classes himself as one of the less-abled players on the American circuit, he feels that he has become far more competitive since his return. “I refer to it more often these days as a sport rather than as a game.”, he says, “It encompasses real head to head heartfelt competition and thoughtful strategy. Don’t get me wrong, I always enjoyed scoring the winning goal against my childhood buddy, but, today it feels more like the competition I enjoyed in my college American football and baseball days.”

“What struck me when I was reintroducing myself to the sport and travelling throughout the Midwest was how much I enjoyed the people and the camaraderie they promote. People here really enjoy being with each other at these tournament gatherings and want to see the sport grow. I would liken many of these gatherings to those long weekends we sometimes have with buddies from our high school and college days who long since moved away.”

Finally, where will the game be in 30 years time? Chris is positive: “I actually think that it will grow as long as there is a continuing strong interest in football world-wide.” The link with football is key, Chris feels, along with an active recruitment effort for younger players via social media and on the ground by local clubs.
